How we work

Principles we don't compromise on.

Every decision we make — from how we file a case to how we price our service — follows from these.

01

Attorney eyes on every filing

We don't automate legal filings. Every takedown is reviewed and signed by a licensed attorney before submission. This makes platforms take us seriously — and it protects you from badly-formed filings that could backfire.

02

Right clause, right platform

Generic DMCA notices don't work on deepfakes. We file under the specific clause that applies — likeness rights, synthetic-media policy, impersonation policy — because that's what gets content removed and accounts terminated.

03

Proof, not promises

Every removal is documented with a timestamped screenshot, logged to your dashboard, and included in your enforcement report. If we file and the platform declines, we tell you — and we explain why and what the next option is.

04

Your money back if we don't deliver

If we can't get you a single removal in the first 30 days, you get a full refund. No forms, no hoops. The free first takedown exists so you see the work before committing. We're comfortable with that bar.

Our story

From a research project to enforcement at scale.

The problem becomes impossible to ignore

Sarah and Marcus, working on opposite sides of the platform-abuse space, notice the same pattern: deepfake impersonation is escalating faster than platform policy can respond, and victims — especially those without large legal teams — have no effective recourse.

DeepGuard is founded

The founding team is assembled: two IP attorneys, a former platform T&S lead, and an AI detection researcher. The first prototype of our monitoring and filing system is tested on a pro-bono basis with five public-figure clients.

DeepGuard reaches 1,000 confirmed takedowns across YouTube, TikTok, and Meta within the first six months. The 91% success rate and attorney-filed model attract clients from entertainment, politics, and religious organizations.
